# The interview

Six things to establish, and how to ask for them. The order is deliberate: each
answer narrows the ones below it.

## How to ask

- **One question at a time.** A menu of four questions gets one answer and three
  shrugs.
- **Recommend a claim and give its reason.** "I think the person in pain here is
  the reviewer, not the author — the author already knows what they changed."
  A neutral prompt gives them nothing to push against; an argued one does, and
  the correction is where the real idea surfaces.
- **Say what the question decides.** Lead with what changes depending on the
  answer.
- **Name a contradiction on the spot.** When an answer collides with an earlier
  one, say so and ask which holds. Nodding at both defers the collision to the
  moment it is expensive.
- **Ask only where the answer would change the document.** When nothing further
  would, stop asking and go write.

## What to establish

### 1. Whose pain, and how bad

A named kind of person in a named situation. "Users" is not an answer, and
neither is "everyone".

Ask for the last time it actually happened. A story has details; a hypothesis
has adjectives.

### 2. How they cope today

Every real pain already has a workaround — a spreadsheet, a colleague, a
tolerated annoyance, or plain endurance. Find it.

An idea with no incumbent is usually an idea with no pain. And the incumbent is
what the idea has to beat, so it sets the bar for everything below.

### 3. What success looks like

One sentence someone could check. "Faster", "smoother", "better" are feelings,
not criteria — push until there is an observable difference between the world
with this thing and the world without it.

### 4. The boundary: what this is deliberately not

Ideas rarely die of being wrong. They die of swelling until nobody can build
them. Ask what they are willing to leave out, and write it down as a promise
rather than an oversight.

### 5. The riskiest assumption

The one that, if false, takes the whole idea down with it — usually a belief
about people, not about feasibility: that they want this, that they will change
a habit, that they will trust it with something that matters.

They will not volunteer it. Propose the one you think is load-bearing and let
them argue; their correction is more honest than their answer to an open
question. This is the question people skip, and it is the one that decides
whether the idea was ever worth having.

### 6. For a new capability on an existing product: why here

Why this product, and not a separate tool or somebody else's. What in the
product already — the audience, the data, the moment in the workflow — makes it
the natural home.

Without an answer, it is an orphan feature: plausible on its own, and unowned
the moment it ships.
